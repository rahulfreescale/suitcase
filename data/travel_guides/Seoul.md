# Seoul, South Korea — Destination Guide

## Overview
Seoul is South Korea's fast, dynamic capital — a city where Joseon-era palaces sit
beneath neon high-rises, K-pop meets centuries-old tradition, and world-class transit
ties it all together. It's huge, modern, safe, and endlessly energetic.

## Best Time to Visit
Spring (April–May, cherry blossoms) and fall (September–November, crisp and colorful)
are the best. Summer is hot, humid, and rainy (monsoon in July); winter is cold and dry
but lively with lights and markets.

## Neighborhoods
Jongno holds the grand palaces and Bukchon Hanok Village. Myeongdong is the shopping and
street-food hub. Gangnam, south of the Han River, is the modern, upscale district.
Hongdae is the youthful arts-and-nightlife area; Itaewon is international and diverse.

## Things to Do
Tour Gyeongbokgung Palace and catch the changing of the guard, wander the traditional
hanok alleys of Bukchon, ride to N Seoul Tower on Namsan, shop and snack in Myeongdong,
stroll the Cheonggyecheon stream and Han River parks, and dress up in hanbok (free palace
entry). Day-trip to the DMZ if time allows.

## Getting Around
Seoul's subway is world-class — vast, cheap, English-signed, and with elevators at every
station. Buses are extensive; taxis are plentiful and affordable. The city is large and
spread out, so transit beats walking between districts, though some historic
neighborhoods are hilly.

## Budget Notes
Seoul is moderate and offers great value: many palaces cost about $2–3 (free in hanbok),
several museums are free, and street food is cheap and excellent. Transit is inexpensive.
Splurges come from Korean BBQ dinners, cafes, and shopping.

## Family & Accessibility
Seoul is one of Asia's most accessible big cities: the subway has elevators at every
station, platform screen doors, wheelchair lifts, and family/nursing facilities, and
Korean culture is warmly welcoming to children. Gyeongbokgung Palace has mostly level,
well-maintained grounds with ramps and accessible restrooms (some rougher cobblestone
patches are avoidable), and the changing-of-the-guard is a toddler hit; kids under 6
enter palaces free, and museums like the National Folk Museum and National Museum of
Korea (with a dedicated children's museum) are free and stroller-accessible. Flat, easy
wins include the Cheonggyecheon stream, the Han River parks, Seoul Forest, and the
Gyeongbokgung Stonewall Walkway. Namdaemun Market is set on a wide, flat road. The main
challenges are the hills and the sheer scale: Bukchon Hanok Village is genuinely steep
(a power wheelchair or fit pusher helps), and getting between far-flung sights means a
lot of transit — not every station's elevator is easy to find, so families often mix in
affordable taxis. Department stores and malls have excellent baby rooms, and there are
kids' cafes everywhere for a break.

## Popular but Challenging
- Bukchon Hanok Village — the iconic traditional-house alleys are on a steep hillside with sloping, uneven lanes; hard with a manual wheelchair or stroller. Alt: the flat Gyeongbokgung Stonewall Walkway nearby for similar atmosphere, or visit with a carrier.
- N Seoul Tower / Namsan approach — the cable-car boarding and some park paths involve stairs; the cable car only recently became accessible via the Namsan Orumi slanted elevator. Alt: use the Orumi elevator route, or enjoy the flat Han River views instead.
- Myeongdong at peak times — tourist-central and exciting, but jam-packed, tight, and not particularly toddler-focused after dark. Alt: base elsewhere with kids; visit off-peak.
- Traditional-market food alleys — amazing eating, but some are narrow and crowded; Namdaemun's main flat road is the easy exception. Alt: stick to the wider market streets.
- Subway at rush hour with a stroller — elevators exist but can be far apart and hard to find, and trains pack tight. Alt: use the subway app to pre-locate lifts, or take an affordable taxi.
