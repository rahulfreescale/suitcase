# Boston, USA — Destination Guide

## Overview
Boston is compact, historic, and walkable — the cradle of the American Revolution, a red-brick city of colonial lanes, elite universities, and a working harbor. Small and dense, it's easily explored on foot, though its oldest quarters are cobbled and hilly.

## Best Time to Visit
Fall (September–October) is glorious with crisp air and foliage; late spring is lovely too. Summer is warm and busy; winter is cold and snowy. Spring and fall are the sweet spots.

## Neighborhoods
Beacon Hill is gaslit cobbled charm; the North End is the Italian food quarter; Back Bay has elegant brownstones and shopping; Downtown holds the Freedom Trail sites; Cambridge across the river is Harvard and MIT; the Seaport is the modern waterfront.

## Things to Do
Walk the Freedom Trail past Revolution-era sites, stroll Boston Common and the Public Garden, eat in the North End, visit the MFA and Isabella Stewart Gardner museums, and cross to Harvard in Cambridge. Catch a Red Sox game at Fenway.

## Getting Around
Boston is small and walkable. The 'T' subway is the oldest in the US and only partly accessible — many stations have elevators but check; buses are fully accessible. The Common, Back Bay and Seaport are flat and smooth; Beacon Hill and the North End are cobbled and hilly, and the Freedom Trail crosses uneven historic ground.

## Budget Notes
Mid-to-expensive, especially lodging. The Freedom Trail, Common, Public Garden and harbor walk are free; the MFA has free/discount windows. The North End's cheap Italian, food halls and Sox bleachers keep some things affordable; museums and hotels are the spend.

## Family & Accessibility
Boston is walkable and flat in its newer quarters (Back Bay, Seaport, the Common and Public Garden), where wheeling and strolling are easy, and the big museums (MFA, Gardner) are fully accessible. But the historic heart is challenging: Beacon Hill and the North End are steep and cobbled, and the Freedom Trail crosses uneven brick and colonial ground. The old 'T' stations have partial elevator coverage. Buses are the reliable step-free fallback. A tale of two cities for access — easy new, hard old.

## Popular but Challenging
- Freedom Trail — A 2.5-mile route past historic sites; much of it follows uneven brick and cobbled sidewalks with hills through Beacon Hill and the North End, and some sites have steps.
- Beacon Hill — Postcard-perfect but built on a steep hill paved in uneven cobblestones and brick with the famous Acorn Street impassable for wheels; very hard for wheelchairs, strollers and seniors.
- North End (Italian quarter) — The beloved food quarter has narrow, hilly, cobbled streets and many restaurants with a step at the door; the food is worth it but access is a squeeze.
