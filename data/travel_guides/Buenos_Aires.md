# Buenos Aires, Argentina — Destination Guide

## Overview
Buenos Aires is Argentina's grand, European-flavored capital — a city of wide boulevards,
Belle Epoque architecture, passionate tango, world-class steak, and a late-night rhythm.
Sophisticated and soulful, it's defined less by single landmarks than by its distinct
neighborhoods.

## Best Time to Visit
Spring (September–November) and fall (March–May) are ideal — mild and pleasant (seasons
are reversed in the southern hemisphere). Summer (December–February) is hot and many
locals leave; winter (June–August) is cool but rarely freezing.

## Neighborhoods
Recoleta is elegant and leafy (home to the famous cemetery). Palermo is the sprawling,
trendy district of parks, boutiques, and restaurants. San Telmo is the atmospheric old
quarter of cobbled streets and antiques. La Boca is the colorful, working-class birthplace
of tango. Puerto Madero is the sleek modern waterfront.

## Things to Do
Pay respects at Recoleta Cemetery, watch tango in San Telmo or La Boca's Caminito, stroll
the Palermo parks and rose garden, see the Teatro Colón and Plaza de Mayo, eat your way
through parrillas (steakhouses) and cafes, and browse the Sunday San Telmo market.

## Getting Around
Buenos Aires has an old but useful Subte (subway), extensive buses (colectivos), and cheap,
plentiful taxis. The city is flat and walkable within neighborhoods, though it sprawls, so
you'll mix walking with transit. A SUBE card covers subway and buses.

## Budget Notes
Buenos Aires can be excellent value, though Argentina's economy is volatile and prices
shift. Steak dinners, cafes, and transit are generally affordable. Many highlights — the
parks, plazas, Recoleta Cemetery, La Boca's streets, and street tango — are free or cheap.

## Family & Accessibility
Buenos Aires is warmly family-friendly and mostly flat, but its accessibility is uneven.
The culture adores children — porteños welcome kids everywhere, playgrounds dot every
neighborhood, dinners run late (toddlers out at 10pm is normal), and restaurants readily
offer high chairs. The Palermo parks, the Rosedal rose garden, Puerto Madero's waterfront,
and the revamped Ecoparque (free) are flat, paved, and stroller-easy, and there are
excellent kids' museums (Museo de los Niños, MIJU with a 0–3 zone). Many modern museums
(like MALBA) and the Floralis Genérica sculpture area are accessible. The real challenge
is the sidewalks: in older neighborhoods they're frequently cracked, narrow, and uneven,
with high curbs and inconsistent ramps — a foldable stroller or carrier helps, and manual
wheelchair users may need assistance. San Telmo and La Boca are cobbled. The Subte is
largely not step-free (old system), so accessible travel leans on buses and taxis. Petty
theft/pickpocketing happens in crowds, so keep bags secure. Overall: a genuinely kid-loving
city that's easy in its parks and modern areas, bumpier in its historic ones.

## Popular but Challenging
- La Boca / Caminito — the colorful tango birthplace is a must-see, but its cobblestones and crowds are hard with a stroller or wheelchair, and the surrounding area needs care after dark. Alt: visit midday, stick to the main Caminito strip, or enjoy tango at an accessible Palermo/Recoleta venue.
- San Telmo's cobbled streets (and Sunday market) — atmospheric but rough cobbles and dense weekend crowds make wheeling tough. Alt: the flat, paved Palermo parks and Puerto Madero for an easier stroll.
- Recoleta Cemetery — iconic, but its narrow lanes between mausoleums are uneven underfoot; partially navigable but bumpy. Alt: the adjacent flat Recoleta plazas and the Floralis Genérica sculpture.
- Older-neighborhood sidewalks generally — not one place but a city-wide issue: cracked, high-curbed, ramp-inconsistent pavements. Alt: use wide avenues and parks, a carrier for toddlers, and taxis to skip bad stretches.
- The Subte (subway) at rush hour — cheap and characterful, but the old system is largely not wheelchair/stroller accessible and gets packed. Alt: flat buses (many low-floor) or inexpensive taxis.
