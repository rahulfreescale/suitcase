# Prague, Czech Republic — Destination Guide

## Overview
Prague is the Czech capital and one of Europe's most beautiful cities — a fairy-tale skyline
of Gothic spires, a hilltop castle, Baroque palaces, and the medieval Charles Bridge over the
Vltava. Remarkably well-preserved and compact, its historic center is a UNESCO treasure.

## Best Time to Visit
Spring (April–June) and fall (September–October) bring pleasant weather and thinner crowds.
Summer is warm but very busy; winter is cold but magical, with famous Christmas markets on
the Old Town Square. Shoulder months balance weather and crowds best.

## Neighborhoods
The Old Town (Staré Město) holds the Astronomical Clock and Old Town Square. The Lesser Town
(Malá Strana) sits below the castle across the river. Hradčany is the Castle Quarter up the
hill. The New Town (Nové Město) has Wenceslas Square and grander 19th-century boulevards.

## Things to Do
Cross the statue-lined Charles Bridge, watch the Astronomical Clock strike the hour on Old
Town Square, tour the vast Prague Castle and St. Vitus Cathedral, wander the Lesser Town and
Jewish Quarter, take in the views from Petřín Hill, and enjoy the beer-hall culture and hearty
Czech food.

## Getting Around
Prague has an excellent, cheap transit network — metro, extensive trams, and buses (tickets by
time, valid across all). Many trams and buses are low-floor and accessible, and most metro
stations have step-free access. The historic center is compact and walkable, though its
cobblestones are the defining catch.

## Budget Notes
Prague is one of Europe's better-value capitals — beer, food, and transit are cheap, and much
of the joy (wandering the bridges, squares, and castle grounds) is free. Castle grounds are
free to enter (buildings ticketed). Foreigner and local prices are similar; it's affordable
overall.

## Family & Accessibility
Prague is beautiful but only moderately accessible, and the reason is underfoot: nearly the
entire historic center is cobblestone, and many sidewalks are small stone tiles — bumpy for
wheelchairs and hard work with a stroller. That said, the city has invested in access and it's
very doable with planning. Transit helps enormously: low-floor trams and buses have ramps, most
metro stations are step-free, and there are accessible taxis. Prague Castle is largely accessible
via the #22 tram plus a ramped route (avoiding the big staircase), with barrier-free courtyards,
the Royal Gardens, and a wheelchair-accessible St. Vitus Cathedral — and entry is free with a
disability ID. Old Town Square and Charles Bridge have improved surfaces and are navigable
(bumpy but doable), and the Prague Zoo lends strollers and wheelchairs free at the entrance.
Czech culture is child-friendly, with beer gardens and parks welcoming families. The challenges:
cobblestones everywhere, some older attractions and quirky small museums are upstairs and
inaccessible, curb cuts are inconsistent, and the castle-hill area involves gradients. For
toddlers, a sturdy stroller (not a lightweight travel one) or a carrier handles the cobbles best,
and the flat parks (Letná, Stromovka) and the zoo are easy wins.

## Popular but Challenging
- Charles Bridge — the iconic must-cross, but its old surface is bumpy and it's insanely crowded by day; tough to wheel and stroller-jostling. Alt: cross early morning or at night when it's quieter, or enjoy river views from an accessible Vltava cruise.
- Old Town lanes and the Astronomical Clock crowds — gorgeous, but rough cobbles plus dense crowds make wheeling slow and hard. Alt: view the clock from the edge of the square, and use trams to skip the roughest stretches.
- Prague Castle's main staircase (Old Castle Stairs) — the classic approach is a long, steep flight of stairs; impossible with wheels. Alt: take tram #22 to the top and use the ramped entrance — most of the castle grounds are then accessible.
- Petřín Hill / Vyšehrad — beautiful viewpoints, but hilly with uneven or stepped paths; the Petřín funicular helps but isn't always fully accessible. Alt: the flat Letná Park beer garden for skyline views instead.
- Quirky small museums (in Old Town townhouses) — many are fun but upstairs with no lift; inaccessible. Alt: choose accessible ones like the Kafka Museum (Malá Strana), which has step-free access.
