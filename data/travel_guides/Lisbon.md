# Lisbon, Portugal — Destination Guide

## Overview
Lisbon is Portugal's hilly, coastal capital, known for pastel buildings, historic
trams, and a relaxed Atlantic pace. It's one of Western Europe's most affordable
capitals, making it popular for budget-conscious and mid-range travelers.

## Best Time to Visit
Spring (March–May) and fall (September–October) offer warm days, fewer crowds,
and lower prices. Summer is hot and busy; winter is mild but rainier.

## Neighborhoods
Alfama is the oldest district — narrow lanes, Fado music, and viewpoints (miradouros).
Baixa is the flat, walkable downtown. Bairro Alto is the nightlife hub. Belém, to
the west, holds the famous monuments and the original pastéis de nata bakery.

## Things to Do
Ride Tram 28 through the old city, visit São Jorge Castle for panoramic views,
explore the Jerónimos Monastery in Belém, and take a day trip to the palaces of
Sintra (40 minutes by train). Food lovers should try bacalhau (salt cod) and
fresh grilled sardines.

## Getting Around
Lisbon is very walkable but steep — comfortable shoes matter. The metro, trams,
and funiculars cover the rest. It's a compact city; most visitors don't need taxis.

## Budget Notes
Lisbon is budget-to-mid-range. Meals are inexpensive by Western European standards,
and public transport is cheap. Belém and Sintra are easy low-cost day trips.

## Family & Accessibility
Lisbon's steep, cobbled hills make stroller and wheelchair travel challenging in Alfama, but the flat Baixa and Parque das Nações areas are much easier. Families enjoy the Oceanário aquarium and tram rides.

## Popular but Challenging
- Tram 28 — the iconic yellow tram is a tourist rite, but the historic cars have steps, cramped interiors, and 45-minute queues; not wheelchair accessible and impractical with a stroller. Alt: take the modern low-floor Tram 15E to flat Belém, or walk the Alfama route with a carrier.
- Alfama & Bairro Alto — the most atmospheric old districts, but a maze of steep cobbled alleys and endless stairs; "stroller-hostile" and very hard in a manual wheelchair. Alt: save Alfama for carrier-only exploration; base and roam in flat Baixa, Belém, or Parque das Nações instead.
- Belém Tower & Monument to the Discoveries — famous riverside landmarks, but the Tower isn't wheelchair accessible and both sit across a highway whose only pedestrian bridge and tunnel are stairs-only. Alt: pre-book an adapted taxi to drop you on the waterfront side (~$6); the nearby Jerónimos Monastery IS fully accessible (free for disabled visitors, side entrance ramp).
- São Jorge Castle — the hilltop castle is reached by steep streets and a non-accessible bus; a genuine climb. Alt: take a taxi up (there's a lift partway near the hospital), or enjoy the accessible Graça viewpoint nearby.
- The seven hills generally — not one place but the defining challenge: steep gradients plus classic Portuguese cobblestones tire everyone and grip poorly on descents. Alt: use the funiculars and metro lifts to skip climbs, and fit a sturdy stroller with air tires (or rent an all-terrain one).
