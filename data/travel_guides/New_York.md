# New York City, USA — Destination Guide

## Overview
New York is America's biggest, fastest, most iconic city — five boroughs of skyscrapers,
world-class museums, Broadway, Central Park, and every cuisine on earth. It's dense,
loud, exhilarating, and endlessly walkable, with an energy unlike anywhere else.

## Best Time to Visit
Spring (April–June) and fall (September–November) offer the best weather and are ideal
for walking. Summer is hot and humid; winter is cold but magical around the holidays.
December's lights and markets are festive but crowded and pricey.

## Neighborhoods
Midtown Manhattan holds Times Square, the Empire State Building, and Broadway. The Upper
West and East Sides flank Central Park and the big museums. Downtown (SoHo, Greenwich
Village, the Financial District) is where the character lives. Brooklyn's waterfront
(DUMBO, Brooklyn Heights) has the skyline views.

## Things to Do
Walk Central Park, see the view from the Empire State Building or Top of the Rock, visit
the Met and the American Museum of Natural History, stroll the High Line, take the Staten
Island Ferry past the Statue of Liberty, catch a Broadway show, and eat your way across
every neighborhood.

## Getting Around
New York runs on its 24/7 subway — fast, cheap, and reaching everywhere — plus buses and
abundant taxis and rideshares. Manhattan's grid is very walkable with wide sidewalks. Kids
under 44 inches ride transit free. The catch for families is that many subway stations
lack elevators.

## Budget Notes
New York is expensive, especially lodging, but many of its best things are free: Central
Park, the High Line, the Staten Island Ferry, walking the Brooklyn Bridge, and window-
shopping. Museums often have suggested-donation or pay-what-you-wish hours. Food spans
dollar pizza slices to fine dining.

## Family & Accessibility
New York is broadly accessible but with one big asterisk: the subway. Manhattan's
sidewalks are wide and flat, the city is intensely walkable, and top attractions are
generally accessible — the major museums (the Met, the Natural History Museum) have
elevators and accessible restrooms, the High Line has elevators at several access points,
and Central Park, Brooklyn Bridge Park, and Hudson River Park have smooth paved paths and
playgrounds. All city buses are wheelchair accessible and allow strollers (sometimes
folded), and kids under 44" ride free. The real challenge is the subway: only around a
third of stations have elevators, and outages happen — wheelchair users and stroller
families must plan routes via the MTA app, and parents frequently carry strollers up
stairs (or use a carrier). Buses are the accessible fallback for cross-town trips. For
toddlers, the city is a lot of stimulation and walking — strollers are worth it for naps
and long days, but expect crowds in Times Square and at peak sights. Restaurants and
museums widely have changing tables and high chairs.

## Popular but Challenging
- The subway system itself — iconic and efficient, but ~two-thirds of stations have no elevator, and many are deep with long staircases; brutal with a stroller and impassable for many wheelchair users. Alt: check the MTA app for accessible stations, or take the fully accessible buses.
- Times Square at peak — a must-see spectacle, but overwhelming crowds, noise, and crush make it hard and stressful with a stroller or wheelchair. Alt: visit early morning, or view briefly and move on.
- Statue of Liberty crown / pedestal — the crown is a narrow, steep, spiral stair with no elevator (inaccessible); even the pedestal is limited. Alt: the free Staten Island Ferry passes Liberty Island with great accessible views.
- Fisherman's-Wharf-style boardwalks and some older attraction entrances — a handful of older venues and cobbled areas (parts of DUMBO, the Stone Street area) are uneven. Alt: stick to main paved routes.
- Cramming boroughs in one day — not a place but a trap: distances are large and subway transfers hard with kids. Alt: cluster sights by neighborhood and build in park/nap breaks.
