# Cusco, Peru — Destination Guide

## Overview
Cusco is the ancient Inca capital high in the Peruvian Andes — a UNESCO city where Inca stonework underpins Spanish colonial churches, cobbled lanes climb to hillside ruins, and the gateway to Machu Picchu buzzes with travelers. Beautiful and historic, it's high, steep, and stony.

## Best Time to Visit
The dry season (May–September) is best for clear skies and trekking, though it's the busy, cooler-nights period. The wet season (November–March) is greener but rainier. Note the altitude (3,400m) requires acclimatization year-round.

## Neighborhoods
The historic center around the Plaza de Armas holds the cathedral, colonial arcades and Inca walls. San Blas above is the steep artisan quarter of galleries and viewpoints; San Pedro has the market; the surrounding hills hold Sacsayhuamán and other ruins; the Sacred Valley extends toward Machu Picchu.

## Things to Do
Take in the Plaza de Armas and cathedral, walk the Inca stone streets and Qorikancha temple, browse the San Pedro Market, climb to Sacsayhuamán, and use Cusco as the base for the Sacred Valley and Machu Picchu. Absorb the fusion of Inca and colonial worlds.

## Getting Around
Cusco's historic center is compact but steep, cobbled, and at high altitude, which makes walking tiring even for the fit; many streets are stepped lanes. There is no metro; taxis and walking cover the center, and tours/trains reach the Sacred Valley and Machu Picchu. The altitude, cobbles, and gradients are the defining challenges; the main plaza is relatively flat.

## Budget Notes
Good value. The city, markets, and plaza are cheap to explore; a tourist ticket (boleto turístico) bundles ruins and sites. Machu Picchu, the trains, and guided treks are the major costs. Local menús and markets are very affordable; upscale hotels and the Machu Picchu logistics are the splurge.

## Family & Accessibility
Cusco is genuinely hard for wheelchair users and those with limited mobility: it sits at 3,400m altitude (which taxes everyone), the historic streets are steep and cobbled with stepped lanes, and the Inca ruins are on hillsides of uneven stone. The Plaza de Armas and some colonial ground floors are relatively flat, a few modern hotels and museums have access, and Machu Picchu itself has limited assisted-access arrangements but is largely stepped. Altitude plus terrain make this one of the most demanding destinations; careful planning and assistance are essential.

## Popular but Challenging
- San Pedro Market — A vibrant covered market that is flat inside but crowded, with tight aisles, thresholds and uneven floors; navigable slowly but a squeeze for wheels and strollers.
- Sacsayhuamán — The great Inca fortress on the hill above Cusco is a vast site of steep grassy slopes and massive uneven stonework reached by a climb; not accessible for wheels, and the altitude compounds it.
- San Blas neighborhood & viewpoint — The artist quarter climbs steep, narrow cobbled and stepped lanes to its viewpoint; charming but very hard for wheels and strenuous at altitude.
- Twelve-Angled Stone / Inca streets — The famous Inca stonework lines steep, narrow cobbled pedestrian streets; viewable while navigating the slopes but the surfaces and gradients are hard for wheels and strollers.
- Machu Picchu (day trip) — The wonder is reached by train and bus, and while there are limited assisted-access arrangements, the citadel is fundamentally a site of steep stone terraces and steps; largely not accessible, though the lower viewpoints can sometimes be reached with significant assistance.
- Sacred Valley (Pisac/Ollantaytambo) — The valley's Inca ruins are dramatic hillside terraces reached by steep stone stairs; the towns have some flat plazas but the sites themselves are not accessible.
