# London, United Kingdom — Destination Guide

## Overview
London is a sprawling world capital where two thousand years of history sit beside glass towers — royal palaces, world-class free museums, leafy parks, and a river that organizes it all. It is vast, and each district feels like its own city.

## Best Time to Visit
May to September brings the warmest, longest days, though it's busiest and priciest. Spring and early autumn are pleasant with thinner crowds. Winter is dark and damp but festive and good value.

## Neighborhoods
The West End (Soho, Covent Garden) is theatre and nightlife; the City is the ancient financial core; South Bank runs museums and river walks along the Thames. Kensington holds the big museums; Shoreditch and Hackney are the creative east; Greenwich sits downriver with maritime history.

## Things to Do
See the Tower of London and Tower Bridge, ride the London Eye, walk through the free British Museum, National Gallery, and Tate Modern, watch the Changing of the Guard at Buckingham Palace, and stroll Hyde Park. Catch a West End show and browse Borough Market.

## Getting Around
The Underground is extensive but many older stations have stairs only — check step-free access maps. Buses are 100% wheelchair-accessible with ramps and are often easier than the Tube. Black cabs are wheelchair-accessible by law. The centre is walkable but distances are deceptive.

## Budget Notes
London is expensive, but the major museums (British Museum, National Gallery, Tate, Natural History) are free. Parks, markets, and river walks cost nothing. Theatre, dining, and attractions like the Eye add up fast; an Oyster/contactless cap keeps transit reasonable.

## Family & Accessibility
London is one of the more accessible big cities, though its Victorian bones show. The bus network is fully step-free and the best way around; the Tube is patchier — roughly a third of stations are step-free, so plan routes with TfL's step-free map. Major museums, the South Bank, and most modern attractions are excellent for wheelchairs and strollers, with lifts and level access. Historic sites like the Tower of London have cobbles and some stairs. Parks are flat and pram-friendly. Most theatres offer accessible seating but need advance booking.

## Popular but Challenging
- Tower of London — Historic fortress with cobbles, spiral stairs and the White Tower not step-free; a partial accessible route exists but the Crown Jewels and walls involve stairs.
- Borough Market — Ground-level and mostly step-free but very crowded and tight between stalls; navigating with wheels or a stroller at peak times is a squeeze.
