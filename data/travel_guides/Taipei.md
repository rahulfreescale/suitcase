# Taipei, Taiwan — Destination Guide

## Overview
Taipei is Taiwan's warm, welcoming capital — a green, mountain-ringed city of night markets, temples, hot springs, and a famous skyscraper, with some of Asia's friendliest streets. Modern, orderly, and largely flat in the center, it's easy and pleasant to navigate.

## Best Time to Visit
Autumn (October–November) and spring (March–April) are the most comfortable — mild and drier. Summer is hot, humid and typhoon-prone; winter is cool and can be rainy. Aim for the shoulder seasons.

## Neighborhoods
Xinyi is the modern downtown around Taipei 101; Zhongshan and Datong hold historic temples and old streets; Ximending is the youthful shopping-and-nightlife zone; Da'an has leafy streets and the big park; Beitou to the north is hot springs; Shilin has the famous night market.

## Things to Do
Ride up Taipei 101, explore the National Palace Museum's treasures, graze the Shilin and Raohe night markets, soak in Beitou's hot springs, visit Longshan Temple, and take the gondola to Maokong's tea houses. Day-trip to Jiufen or Yangmingshan.

## Getting Around
The Taipei MRT is superb — clean, cheap, and fully accessible with lifts, tactile paving, level boarding and priority everything; buses are accessible too. The central districts are flat and walkable with wide sidewalks and covered arcades. The surrounding mountains and old streets (Jiufen) are steep, but the city itself is easy.

## Budget Notes
Excellent value. Night markets, temples, parks and hikes are cheap or free, and street food is famously affordable and delicious. Taipei 101's observatory, the gondola and nicer dining are the bigger costs, but overall Taipei is very budget-friendly.

## Family & Accessibility
Taipei is one of Asia's most accessible cities: the MRT is fully step-free with excellent facilities and staff, the central districts are flat with wide sidewalks, and modern attractions (Taipei 101, the National Palace Museum, malls) have lifts throughout. Night markets are flat but very crowded and tight. Temples often have a small threshold step. The mountain areas and old towns like Jiufen are steep and stepped. Around the city core, wheels and strollers do very well.

## Popular but Challenging
- Shilin Night Market — A huge, flat but intensely crowded night market with tight lanes and a partly basement food court reached by stairs/lift; navigable slowly but a real squeeze at peak.
- Raohe Street Night Market — A famous flat single-street night market that is step-free but extremely crowded and tight; manageable slowly off-peak.
- Elephant Mountain (Xiangshan) — The classic Taipei 101 viewpoint is reached by a steep staircase hike with no accessible route; not suitable for wheels.
