# Tokyo, Japan — Destination Guide

## Overview
Tokyo is a vast, ultra-modern metropolis layered over deep tradition — neon
districts beside quiet shrines. It rewards both foodies and culture seekers, and
runs on one of the world's best public transit systems.

## Best Time to Visit
Spring (cherry blossoms, late March–April) and fall (October–November, crisp and
colorful) are ideal. Summer is hot and humid; winter is cold but clear.

## Neighborhoods
Shibuya and Shinjuku are the bright, busy hearts. Asakusa holds the historic
Senso-ji temple. Ginza is upscale shopping. Akihabara is electronics and anime.
Shimokitazawa and Nakameguro are the hip, low-key districts.

## Things to Do
Cross the Shibuya Scramble, visit Senso-ji temple in Asakusa, experience teamLab
digital art, day-trip to Nikko or Hakone (for Mt. Fuji views), and eat everything
— sushi, ramen, tempura, izakaya small plates.

## Getting Around
The train and metro network is exceptional; get a rechargeable Suica/Pasmo card.
Trains stop around midnight. Tokyo is safe and orderly but large — plan by area.

## Budget Notes
Tokyo is mid-to-high range but flexible: convenience-store meals and ramen are
cheap, while sushi omakase and hotels run high. Transit is efficient and moderate.

## Family & Accessibility
Tokyo is very family-friendly and largely accessible — most train stations have elevators and staff assistance. Kids love teamLab, Ueno Zoo, and themed cafés. Strollers are easy on the transit system.

## Popular but Challenging
- Rush-hour trains — Tokyo's rail is superb and mostly step-free (elevators at most stations), but during rush hour (roughly 7:30–9:30am and 5–7pm) trains are so densely packed that boarding with an open stroller is genuinely impossible and unsafe. Alt: travel off-peak; board the first/last cars which have wheelchair/stroller space; never rush the gap.
- Station transfers (Shinjuku, Shibuya) — the metro is accessible but lines aren't always connected, and finding the one elevator in a giant station like Shinjuku can add 30 minutes and long detours. Alt: pre-map elevator exits (station apps show them), and build extra transfer time.
- Sensō-ji & Nakamise (Asakusa) — Tokyo's oldest temple is genuinely accessible (a lift on the left reaches the main hall, accessible toilets), but the Nakamise approach and the whole Asakusa area are among the most crowded in Japan, so you get swept along and shops are hard to enter. Alt: the temple itself is a must; visit early/weekday, and use the quieter back alleys to move around.
- Shibuya, Harajuku & Omotesando slopes — trend-central, but parts are surprisingly hilly (Omotesando and NW Shibuya especially), tough in a manual wheelchair, and Shibuya Crossing is a crush. Alt: use the accessible department stores and the flat Shibuya Sky viewpoint; take the hills slowly.
- Temples & shrines generally — a note: many have steps and gravel approaches. Alt: Tokyo's big flat parks (Shinjuku Gyoen, Ueno) and modern complexes (Roppongi Hills) are easy wins between the trickier historic sites.
