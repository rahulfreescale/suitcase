# Bangkok, Thailand — Destination Guide

## Overview
Bangkok is a high-energy Southeast Asian capital famous for street food, ornate
temples, riverside life, and a legendary night scene — all at very low prices.

## Best Time to Visit
The cool, dry season (November–February) is most comfortable. March–May is very
hot; the monsoon (June–October) brings heavy but short downpours.

## Neighborhoods
The Old City (Rattanakosin) holds the Grand Palace and Wat Pho. Sukhumvit is
modern malls and nightlife. Chinatown (Yaowarat) is a street-food paradise.
Khao San Road is the backpacker hub.

## Things to Do
Visit the Grand Palace and Wat Pho's reclining Buddha, take a longtail boat through
the canals, eat your way through Yaowarat at night, and browse Chatuchak weekend
market. Day-trip to Ayutthaya's ancient ruins.

## Getting Around
The BTS Skytrain and MRT metro beat the famous traffic. River ferries are scenic
and cheap. Tuk-tuks are fun but negotiate fares first.

## Budget Notes
Bangkok is a budget traveler's dream — street meals cost a dollar or two, and
transit and lodging are inexpensive. It's easy to eat and stay very well for little.

## Family & Accessibility
Bangkok's sidewalks and crowds can be tough for strollers and wheelchairs, but Skytrain stations have elevators. Families enjoy Safari World and the many malls with kids' zones. Street food suits all ages.

## Popular but Challenging
- The Grand Palace & Wat Phra Kaew — a must-see, but the temple interiors and many halls sit up stairs with no step-free route, and it's mobbed with tour groups by 9:30am; the outer courtyards are accessible (ramps, though some are steep) but bumpy in older stone-paved sections. Alt: enjoy the accessible courtyards early morning, or visit the fully wheelchair-accessible Wat Traimit (Golden Buddha, has a lift) instead.
- Chatuchak Weekend Market — the world's largest weekend market and a highlight, but notoriously poor for wheels: few curb cuts, packed narrow aisles, and open internal roads that squeeze pedestrians. Alt: quieter, wider markets like the Phueng floating market, or go at opening time.
- The tuk-tuk / long-tail boat experience — iconic, but tuk-tuks and most river boats require steps and lifting; only a few Chao Phraya Express boats have an accessible space. Alt: use the accessible Chao Phraya Express boats to reach riverside temples.
- BTS/MRT transfers — the trains are accessible with lifts, but the BTS Skytrain and MRT metro aren't seamlessly connected, so a transfer can mean exiting, buying a new ticket, and a long detour. Alt: plan around single-line trips, or use taxis for cross-system hops.
- Midday temple-hopping — not one place but a hazard: 35°C+ heat plus a strict cover-shoulders-and-knees dress code make midday exhausting for toddlers and wheelchair users. Alt: temples early, air-conditioned malls or the accessible aquarium midday.
