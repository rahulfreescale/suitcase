# Istanbul, Turkey — Destination Guide

## Overview
Istanbul straddles two continents across the Bosphorus, layering Byzantine and Ottoman empires beneath a vast, hilly, 16-million-person metropolis. Domes and minarets, grand bazaars, and ferry horns define a city that is endlessly dramatic — and endlessly hilly.

## Best Time to Visit
April to May and September to November bring mild, pleasant weather and manageable crowds. Summer is hot and humid; winter is cold, grey and can be snowy, but atmospheric and cheaper.

## Neighborhoods
Sultanahmet is the historic peninsula — Hagia Sophia, Blue Mosque, Topkapı. Beyoğlu across the Golden Horn holds İstiklal Street, Galata and nightlife. Kadıköy and Üsküdar sit on the Asian side, more local and relaxed; Beşiktaş and Nişantaşı are modern and upscale.

## Things to Do
Marvel at Hagia Sophia and the Blue Mosque, tour Topkapı Palace, wander the Grand and Spice Bazaars, cruise the Bosphorus, climb Galata Tower, and soak in a historic hammam. Eat your way through kebabs, meze, and baklava.

## Getting Around
Istanbul is steep and sprawling, but modern trams, metro lines, funiculars and ferries are increasingly accessible with lifts and level boarding — the tram through Sultanahmet is handy and step-free. However, the historic quarters are very hilly with cobbles, steps and uneven pavements. Ferries are a scenic, accessible way to cross.

## Budget Notes
Generally good value, though headline sites have raised foreign-visitor prices. Mosques are free, bazaars are free to wander, and street food (simit, balık ekmek) is cheap. Palace and museum tickets and the Bosphorus tours cost more; bargaining is expected in bazaars.

## Family & Accessibility
Istanbul is hard for wheelchair users: it is built on steep hills with cobblestones, uneven pavements and countless steps, especially in historic Sultanahmet and Galata. That said, the modern transit (trams, metro, funiculars, ferries) is largely step-free and a real help, and the major mosques and some palaces have added ramps and accessible entrances. Crowds at the bazaars and mosques are intense. Strollers and wheelchairs are workable along the flat tram spine and waterfronts but struggle in the hilly old lanes.

## Popular but Challenging
- Topkapı Palace — A sprawling palace with cobbled courtyards, thresholds, steps between pavilions and the Harem's stairs; some accessible routes and ramps exist but much is uneven and hilly.
- Grand Bazaar — Mostly flat covered lanes but extremely crowded, with worn uneven flooring and thresholds at entrances; navigable in parts but a real squeeze with wheels or a stroller.
- Spice Bazaar — Flat single covered hall but very tight and crowded; entrances have small steps or thresholds.
- Basilica Cistern — The atmospheric underground cistern is reached by a staircase down; recent renovations added some access but it remains largely stair-based and damp.
