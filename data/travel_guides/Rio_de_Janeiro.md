# Rio de Janeiro, Brazil — Destination Guide

## Overview
Rio is Brazil's spectacular seaside city — golden beaches curving beneath forested granite peaks, a giant Christ statue watching over it all, and a rhythm of samba, sun, and celebration. Stunning and sensual, it's also steep, with mountains rising straight from the sand.

## Best Time to Visit
December to March is hot, vibrant summer (and Carnival in Feb/Mar) but crowded and pricey. May to October is cooler, drier and more comfortable for sightseeing. Aim for the shoulder months to balance weather and crowds.

## Neighborhoods
Copacabana and Ipanema are the famous beach neighborhoods, flat along the sand. Leblon is upscale beachfront; Santa Teresa is the hilly bohemian artists' quarter; Lapa is nightlife and the arches; Centro holds colonial and modern landmarks; Botafogo and Flamengo curve around the bay toward Sugarloaf.

## Things to Do
Ride up to Christ the Redeemer on Corcovado, take the cable car up Sugarloaf Mountain, relax on Copacabana and Ipanema beaches, stroll the Selarón Steps and Santa Teresa, watch sunset from Arpoador, and feel the samba in Lapa. Time it for Carnival if you can handle the crowds.

## Getting Around
Rio's beach neighborhoods are flat and walkable along wide seaside promenades, and the metro is modern and accessible with lifts at most stations; buses are accessible too. But the city is defined by steep hills — Santa Teresa, the favelas, and the mountain attractions — and beach sand is a barrier. The flat beachfronts and metro make coastal Rio manageable; the heights need cable cars, trains, or cars.

## Budget Notes
Mid-range with wide variation. The beaches, promenades, Selarón Steps and Arpoador sunset are free; Christ the Redeemer and Sugarloaf have significant ticket costs. Street food, juice bars and botecos are cheap; Carnival and upscale dining are the splurges.

## Family & Accessibility
Rio is a mix. The flat beach promenades of Copacabana and Ipanema are wide, paved and step-free — excellent for wheeling and strolling — and the metro is accessible, as are many modern venues. The beaches themselves have accessible mats and beach wheelchairs at some points. But the signature attractions are on peaks: Christ the Redeemer is reached by an accessible cog train and then lifts/escalators to near the statue (largely accessible), and Sugarloaf's cable cars are accessible, but Santa Teresa, the Selarón Steps and the historic hill quarters are steep and stepped. Coastal Rio is manageable; the heights vary.

## Popular but Challenging
- Selarón Steps (Escadaria Selarón) — The famous mosaic staircase is, by nature, a long flight of steps with no accessible route; you can view the base from the flat street but not ascend.
- Santa Teresa — The bohemian hilltop neighborhood is steep and cobbled with the historic tram and stepped lanes; charming but very hard for wheels and strollers.
