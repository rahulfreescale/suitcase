# Cape Town, South Africa — Destination Guide

## Overview
Cape Town sits between Table Mountain and the Atlantic — a scenic city of beaches,
vineyards, dramatic coastline, and rich history.

## Best Time to Visit
Summer (November–March) is warm, dry, and best for beaches and hiking. Winter
(June–August) is cooler and wetter but greener and quieter.

## Neighborhoods
The City Bowl sits beneath Table Mountain. The V&A Waterfront is the tourist hub.
Camps Bay and Clifton are beach suburbs. Bo-Kaap is the colorful, historic Cape
Malay quarter. Constantia is wine country.

## Things to Do
Take the cable car (or hike) up Table Mountain, visit Cape Point and the Cape of
Good Hope, see penguins at Boulders Beach, tour the Constantia or Stellenbosch
wineries, and learn history on a Robben Island tour.

## Getting Around
A rental car is the most practical for the scattered sights and coastal drives.
Ride-hailing works well in the city; public transit is limited for visitors.

## Budget Notes
Mid-range and often good value for international visitors. Wine, dining, and
activities are reasonably priced; guided tours and car rental are the main costs.

## Family & Accessibility
Cape Town is best explored by car with kids, given spread-out sights. Boulders Beach penguins and the aquarium are family highlights. The Table Mountain cableway is wheelchair accessible; many trails are not.

## Popular but Challenging
- Table Mountain summit paths — the cableway itself is accessible (rotating cars, and wheelchair users skip the queue), but the mountaintop has rough, occasionally steep paths and one stairs section that forces the long way around, and it closes without notice on windy days. Alt: do the accessible loop on a clear day, book online, arrive early; enjoy the views without attempting the rougher trails.
- Robben Island — the ferry crossing and the bus/prison tour are accessible, but the island has unpaved stretches and real distances between points; vague "it's accessible" assurances mislead. Alt: ask specific questions when booking (surfaces, distances, seating) and pace it.
- Cape Point paths — the Flying Dutchman Funicular makes the upper viewpoint reachable, but the surrounding paths aren't all paved and it needs private/adapted transport to reach. Alt: use the funicular and stick to the paved viewpoints.
- Signal Hill sunset — a favourite viewpoint, but no city or sightseeing bus goes there, so it needs a car or taxi. Alt: pre-arrange accessible transport for the sunset run.
- City pavements generally — a real contrast city: world-class precincts (V&A Waterfront is flat and excellent) sit beside crumbling, curb-cut-poor pavements, and MyCiti buses are accessible but broken ramps are a recurring issue. Alt: base around the V&A Waterfront and use accessible taxis for gaps.
