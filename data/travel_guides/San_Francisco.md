# San Francisco, USA — Destination Guide

## Overview
San Francisco is a scenic, foggy, famously hilly city on a peninsula between bay and
ocean — cable cars, Victorian houses, the Golden Gate Bridge, and a compact patchwork of
distinct neighborhoods. It's beautiful and walkable in parts, punishingly steep in others.

## Best Time to Visit
Fall (September–October) is the warmest and clearest — the real San Francisco summer.
Actual summer (June–August) is often cold and foggy ("Karl the Fog"). Spring is pleasant;
winter is mild but rainy. Always pack layers; the microclimates shift block to block.

## Neighborhoods
Fisherman's Wharf and the Embarcadero are the flat, tourist-friendly waterfront. Nob Hill
and Russian Hill are the steep, classic-view districts. The Mission is sunny, foodie, and
vibrant. Golden Gate Park anchors the west; Chinatown and North Beach are dense and
walkable (if hilly).

## Things to Do
See the Golden Gate Bridge, tour Alcatraz, ride a cable car, wander Golden Gate Park (the
California Academy of Sciences is a family favorite), eat through the Ferry Building and
Chinatown, watch the sea lions at Pier 39, and take in the views from Twin Peaks. Day trips
to Muir Woods or wine country are easy.

## Getting Around
The Muni system (buses, light rail, historic streetcars) is almost entirely wheelchair
accessible and, with BART, covers the city and region. The famous cable cars are NOT
accessible. The city is walkable where it's flat (the Embarcadero, the Marina) but has some
of the steepest hills in the country. Rideshares and taxis fill the gaps.

## Budget Notes
San Francisco is expensive, especially hotels. But many highlights are free: walking the
Golden Gate Bridge, Golden Gate Park and its gardens, the Ferry Building, Pier 39, and the
Cable Car Museum. Alcatraz and the Academy of Sciences charge admission. Sourdough, dim sum,
and the Mission's taquerias keep eating affordable.

## Family & Accessibility
San Francisco is a paradox: it's one of the hilliest cities anywhere, yet also genuinely
accessible thanks to wide sidewalks, curb ramps, and a nearly-all-accessible Muni system
(every Muni bus, train, and streetcar is wheelchair accessible; the cable cars are not).
The trick is choosing flat areas and letting transit handle the hills. Flat, easy wins
include the Embarcadero (level for miles), Fisherman's Wharf and Pier 39, the Ferry
Building, Crissy Field, and much of Golden Gate Park (wide paved paths, ramps; the
California Academy of Sciences and Conservatory of Flowers are accessible, and you can
borrow/rent a wheelchair at the de Young). The Golden Gate Bridge's pedestrian walkway is
smooth and gradual. Alcatraz is accessible — the ferry has boarding ramps and the island
has a tram up the steep hill (wheelchair users transfer) plus an elevator in the prison.
The challenges are real: Nob Hill, Russian Hill, parts of North Beach, Downtown, and
Pacific Heights have formidable inclines that are exhausting or impassable with a manual
wheelchair or stroller, and the beloved cable cars are old, packed, and not
wheelchair-accessible (hard even with a stroller). For toddlers, base yourself somewhere
level (the Wharf/Embarcadero) and lean on Muni. Layers matter — the fog makes it cold.

## Popular but Challenging
- Cable cars — the quintessential SF experience, but the historic cars are not wheelchair accessible and are usually packed with steep steps to board; very hard with a stroller. Alt: the accessible historic F-line streetcars along the Embarcadero, or Muni buses.
- Lombard Street ("the crookedest street") — famous but on a brutally steep hill; a real climb with a stroller or wheelchair. Alt: view it from the flat bottom, or skip.
- Nob Hill / Russian Hill / Filbert Steps — gorgeous views but among the steepest terrain in the US; largely impassable with a manual wheelchair or stroller. Alt: take Muni or a rideshare up, or choose flat waterfront neighborhoods.
- Japanese Tea Garden (Golden Gate Park) — beautiful but not fully accessible; several areas are stairs-only and strollers can get stuck. Alt: the flat, accessible Conservatory of Flowers and Academy of Sciences nearby.
- Alcatraz island climb — the site is accessible via a tram, but manual wheelchair users may struggle on the quarter-mile hill if the tram doesn't suit them, and it can be cold and windy. Alt: use the island tram and dress warmly.
