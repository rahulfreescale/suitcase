# Dubai, UAE — Destination Guide

## Overview
Dubai is the UAE's futuristic showpiece — a desert metropolis of record-breaking
skyscrapers, colossal malls, artificial islands, and old-world souks along a tidal creek.
Ultramodern, sun-baked, and built for superlatives, it pairs glitz with a surprising layer
of heritage.

## Best Time to Visit
November to March is the season — warm, sunny, and pleasant. Avoid the extreme summer
(May–September), when temperatures regularly exceed 40°C and outdoor life retreats indoors
to the air-conditioned malls.

## Neighborhoods
Downtown Dubai holds the Burj Khalifa, Dubai Mall, and Fountain. Dubai Marina and JBR are
the glossy waterfront high-rise districts. Deira and Bur Dubai, along the Creek, are the
older, atmospheric heart with the souks. Jumeirah has the beaches and the Burj Al Arab.

## Things to Do
Ascend the Burj Khalifa, watch the Dubai Fountain, shop and skate at the Dubai Mall, wander
the Gold and Spice Souks and take an abra across the Creek, stroll the Al Fahidi historic
district, hit the beach at Kite Beach or JBR, and take a desert safari over the dunes.

## Getting Around
The Dubai Metro is driverless, spotless, cheap, and fully accessible, linking the airport,
Downtown, and Marina, backed by trams, buses, and abundant taxis (including accessible
"People of Determination" cabs). The city is car-oriented and spread out, and the heat
makes walking impractical much of the year — plan around air-conditioned transit.

## Budget Notes
Dubai spans budget to ultra-luxury. Many signature experiences are free: the Dubai Fountain
show, the souks, the beaches, and window-shopping the malls. Costs climb with the Burj
Khalifa deck, fine dining, and nightlife. Street food and the metro keep budgets in check.

## Family & Accessibility
Dubai is one of the most accessible cities anywhere — a direct benefit of being almost
entirely new, built with universal design (the UAE honors disabled visitors as "People of
Determination"). The Metro is 100% accessible (elevators at every station, level boarding,
dedicated spaces), and there are accessible taxis on call. The marquee sights are engineered
for access: the Dubai Mall offers miles of flat marble and free wheelchair loans, the Burj
Khalifa's observation decks are step-free with accessible routes, and the Dubai Fountain
promenade and boardwalk are flat and smooth. Miracle Garden's paths are flat (free entry for
People of Determination), and Kite Beach and others offer access ramps and beach mats. Malls
are the family lifeline — vast, air-conditioned, with nursing rooms, play zones, and
attractions (aquarium, ice rink) — a genuine relief with toddlers in the heat. The dominant
challenge is exactly that heat: from roughly May to September, outdoor sightseeing is
punishing and risky for young children, so plan indoors midday. Minor caveats: some metro
stations lack curb drops nearby (curbs can be high), the old souks have narrower, occasionally
uneven lanes, and distances are large so you'll rely on transit. Overall, remarkably smooth
for strollers and wheelchairs.

## Popular but Challenging
- Old souks (Gold & Spice Souk, Deira) — atmospheric and a highlight, but the lanes are narrow, crowded, and occasionally uneven, with an abra (small boat) creek crossing that isn't wheelchair/unfolded-stroller friendly. Alt: the modern, fully accessible Souk Madinat Jumeirah for a similar vibe, or visit off-peak.
- Desert safari dune-bashing — a signature experience, but the 4x4 dune ride and soft sand are hard for wheelchair users and intense for toddlers. Alt: choose an operator with accessible vehicles and a calmer desert-camp experience, or skip the dune-bashing portion.
- Midday outdoor sights (May–Sept) — not a place but a serious hazard: the summer heat is dangerous for young children and exhausting for everyone outdoors. Alt: outdoor activities early morning or evening; malls and indoor attractions in the heat of the day.
- Mall of the Emirates Ski Dubai — fun to watch, but the snow slopes themselves aren't wheelchair accessible. Alt: view from the mall (accessible) and enjoy the many accessible mall attractions.
- High curbs near some metro stations — the metro is superb, but a few stations lack nearby curb cuts, stranding wheelchairs between station and street. Alt: use an accessible taxi door-to-door for those gaps.
