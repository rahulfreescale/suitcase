# Vancouver, Canada — Destination Guide

## Overview
Vancouver is a coastal Pacific Northwest city where ocean, mountains, and forest
meet a diverse, food-loving metropolis. It's clean, green, and outdoorsy.

## Best Time to Visit
Summer (June–September) is warm, dry, and best for the outdoors. Fall and spring
are mild but rainier; winter is wet in the city and snowy in the nearby mountains.

## Neighborhoods
Downtown and the West End border Stanley Park. Gastown is the historic, trendy
quarter. Yaletown is upscale dining. Kitsilano ("Kits") has beaches and a laid-back
vibe. Granville Island is a market and arts hub.

## Things to Do
Cycle the Stanley Park seawall, cross the Capilano Suspension Bridge, ride the
gondola up Grouse Mountain, explore Granville Island Market, and eat across the
city's excellent Asian and seafood scenes. Whistler is a 2-hour day trip.

## Getting Around
Walkable downtown with a good SkyTrain and bus network. A car helps for the North
Shore mountains and Whistler. Cycling infrastructure is excellent.

## Budget Notes
High-range. Lodging and dining are pricey, especially in summer. Many of the best
experiences — parks, beaches, seawall, hikes — are free or low-cost.

## Family & Accessibility
Vancouver is very family- and accessibility-friendly: flat seawall paths, accessible transit, and Stanley Park, the aquarium, and Science World for kids. Most major attractions accommodate wheelchairs and strollers.

## Popular but Challenging
- Capilano Suspension Bridge — one of Vancouver's iconic sights, but the swaying suspension bridge itself is not wheelchair accessible (and younger kids may need a carrier on it); the surrounding park has accessible areas and offers free entry to wheelchair users. Alt: enjoy the accessible park facilities and viewpoints; for a step-free canopy experience, the treetop-style walkways at some parks or Grouse's accessible areas work better.
- Grouse Mountain summit activities — the Skyride gondola is accessible and the views are superb, but the marquee summit activities (ziplining, paragliding, mountain ropes) are not wheelchair accessible, and the paved trail runs out quickly. Alt: ride up for the views, wildlife refuge, and shows; in winter, Grouse partners with Vancouver Adaptive Snow Sports for adaptive skiing.
- Lynn Canyon — a beautiful free alternative to Capilano, but its trails are rougher: doable with an all-terrain stroller, challenging with an umbrella stroller, and not fully wheelchair accessible. Alt: the flat, paved Stanley Park Seawall or Deer Lake loop for easy nature.
- Aquabus / False Creek ferries — cute and stroller-friendly on the larger vessels, but some stops have steep gangways and stairs with no accessible route up. Alt: check which stops are step-free (Granville Island is good) before relying on the ferry.
- Some hills & the rain — a general note: the flat core (downtown, Seawall, Stanley Park, Granville Island) is stroller/wheelchair paradise, but neighbourhoods away from the water have hills, and it rains October–April. Alt: base near the waterfront, use the fully accessible SkyTrain, and keep indoor options (Science World, Aquarium — both accessible) for wet days.
