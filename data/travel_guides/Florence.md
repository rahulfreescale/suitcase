# Florence, Italy — Destination Guide

## Overview
Florence is the compact, walkable cradle of the Renaissance — a dense core of domes, palaces, and world-defining art on the Arno, ringed by Tuscan hills. Small enough to cross on foot, it packs more masterpieces per street than almost anywhere.

## Best Time to Visit
April to June and September to October bring warm, pleasant weather and slightly thinner crowds. July and August are hot and packed. Winter is cool, quiet, and the best time to have the art to yourself.

## Neighborhoods
The historic centre around the Duomo, Piazza della Signoria and the Uffizi is the walkable core. The Oltrarno across the river holds the Pitti Palace, artisan workshops and a more local feel. San Lorenzo has the market; Santa Croce is lively; the hills toward Fiesole and Piazzale Michelangelo offer views.

## Things to Do
See the Duomo and climb (or admire) its dome, tour the Uffizi and Accademia (David), cross the Ponte Vecchio, explore the Pitti Palace and Boboli Gardens, and climb to Piazzale Michelangelo for sunset over the city. Eat bistecca and gelato.

## Getting Around
Florence's centre is tiny and best walked, but it is paved in hard, uneven stone and can be crowded and narrow. There is little metro; buses and trams serve the outskirts and are accessible. The historic core is flat-ish along the river but the paving and crowds are the main accessibility challenge; the hill viewpoints are steep.

## Budget Notes
Mid-to-expensive for sights — the Uffizi, Accademia and Duomo complex all charge and book out; reserve ahead. But wandering the centre, crossing Ponte Vecchio and climbing to Piazzale Michelangelo are free. Trattorias and gelato are reasonable; the museums are the big spend.

## Family & Accessibility
Florence is compact and mostly flat in the centre, which helps, but it is paved throughout in hard, uneven, often-slippery stone that is jarring for wheels and strollers, and the crowds in the core are heavy. The major museums (Uffizi, Accademia) are accessible with lifts, free disabled entry and priority booking, and the Duomo's cathedral floor is accessible though its dome and towers are not. The hill viewpoints and many churches have steps. With planning the art is reachable; the streets themselves are the challenge.

## Popular but Challenging
- Boboli Gardens — A large hillside Renaissance garden with steep gravel paths and stairs; only limited flat sections near the entrance are manageable.
- Bardini Garden viewpoint — A quieter hillside garden with wisteria and views; terraced and stepped with steep paths, only partly accessible near the top entrance.
