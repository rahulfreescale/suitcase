# Hanoi, Vietnam — Destination Guide

## Overview
Hanoi is Vietnam's atmospheric thousand-year-old capital — a dense, motorbike-swirled maze of an Old Quarter, tree-lined French boulevards, lakes, and temples. Layered and characterful, it's flat but famously chaotic and tight underfoot.

## Best Time to Visit
Autumn (September–November) and spring (March–April) are the most pleasant — mild and drier. Summer is hot, humid and wet; winter is cool and can be grey and damp. The shoulder months are best.

## Neighborhoods
The Old Quarter is the ancient tangle of trade streets around Hoan Kiem Lake. The French Quarter to the south has wide boulevards, the Opera House and grand hotels; the Ba Dinh district holds the Ho Chi Minh Mausoleum and Temple of Literature; West Lake (Tay Ho) is leafier and expat-favored.

## Things to Do
Circle Hoan Kiem Lake and its temple, get lost in the Old Quarter's 36 streets, pay respects at the Ho Chi Minh Mausoleum, visit the Temple of Literature and the Ethnology Museum, watch water puppets, and eat pho, bun cha and egg coffee. Day-trip to Halong Bay or Ninh Binh.

## Getting Around
Hanoi is flat but intensely crowded, with narrow, obstructed, often-parked-on footpaths and a torrent of motorbikes that makes crossing streets an art. There's a single elevated metro line (accessible with lifts) but limited coverage; app cabs, taxis and cyclos handle most trips. The lake promenades are walkable; the Old Quarter's tight, uneven lanes are hard.

## Budget Notes
Very cheap. Street food, temples, lake walks and markets cost very little; entries to sights are modest. Halong Bay cruises, nicer hotels and Western dining are the bigger costs, but Hanoi is one of Asia's best-value cities overall.

## Family & Accessibility
Hanoi is hard for wheelchair users: footpaths are narrow, uneven and blocked by parked motorbikes and vendors, street-crossing is chaotic, and many temples and old buildings have steps and thresholds. The single metro line and some modern museums are accessible, the flat lake promenades are wheelable, and the wide French Quarter boulevards are easier than the Old Quarter's tangle. The Ho Chi Minh complex has some flat accessible areas. Overall, expect to rely on cabs and to need assistance at most sights.

## Popular but Challenging
- Old Quarter (36 Streets) — The historic trading maze is flat but the footpaths are narrow, broken and blocked by motorbikes and stalls, forcing you into the busy road; very difficult for wheels and strollers.
- Dong Xuan Market — The city's largest covered market is flat but densely packed with tight aisles, thresholds and crowds; hard to navigate with wheels or a stroller.
