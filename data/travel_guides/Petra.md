# Petra, Jordan — Destination Guide

## Overview
Petra is Jordan's rose-red ancient city carved into desert canyons — a UNESCO wonder of tombs, temples, and the iconic Treasury reached through a narrow gorge. It's an archaeological marvel set in rugged, dramatic terrain that rewards those who can walk far.

## Best Time to Visit
Spring (March–May) and autumn (September–November) are ideal — warm days, cool nights, comfortable for the long walking. Summer is very hot and exposed; winter is cold and can be wet or even snowy.

## Neighborhoods
The modern town of Wadi Musa sits at the entrance with hotels and restaurants and steps down toward the site. The archaeological park itself unfolds from the visitor center through the Siq gorge to the Treasury and out across a vast valley of tombs, with the Monastery high above.

## Things to Do
Walk the Siq to the breathtaking Treasury, explore the Royal Tombs and the Roman street, climb to the Monastery (Ad Deir) or the High Place of Sacrifice for views, and experience Petra by Night by candlelight. Nearby Wadi Rum and Little Petra extend the adventure.

## Getting Around
Petra is a vast outdoor archaeological site covering steep, rugged desert terrain; distances are long and the ground is sand, gravel, uneven rock and stairs. From the visitor center it's about 2km through the Siq to the Treasury alone. Horse carriages run the entry stretch, and the main trail to the Treasury has been partly graded, but most of the site is not accessible. Wadi Musa town is steep.

## Budget Notes
The entry ticket is significant (the Jordan Pass bundles it with the visa and other sites and is the best value). Inside, carriages, donkeys and guides cost extra. Wadi Musa has a range of hotels and cheap local food; the site fee is the main expense.

## Family & Accessibility
Petra is inherently difficult for wheelchair users and those with limited mobility — it's a vast desert site of sand, gravel, rock steps and long distances. However, real accommodations exist: the authorities run an accessibility program, the main path from the entrance through the Siq to the Treasury has been graded and can be done with assistance or by golf-cart (electric carts are available on request for visitors with disabilities), and horse carriages cover the entry stretch. Beyond the Treasury, the Royal Tombs, the Monastery climb (900+ steps) and the High Place are not accessible. The Treasury view is achievable with the cart/assistance; the deeper site is not.

## Popular but Challenging
- The Siq — The dramatic 1.2km entry gorge has been partly graded to a mostly firm surface and can be done with an electric cart or assistance, though there are uneven cobbled and sandy stretches.
- The Treasury (Al-Khazneh) — The iconic facade is reached at the end of the Siq on a sandy, mostly level forecourt; electric carts can bring visitors with disabilities to this point.
- The Monastery (Ad Deir) — Reached only by a strenuous climb of roughly 800–900 rock-cut steps up the mountain; no accessible route whatsoever.
- Royal Tombs — A row of monumental tombs set above the valley, reached by uneven rock steps and slopes from the main trail; not accessible for wheels.
- Street of Facades & Roman Theatre — The wide valley floor beyond the Treasury is sand and gravel with the carved facades and theatre alongside; firm-ish in stretches but uneven and long, hard for wheels without a cart.
- Colonnaded Street & Great Temple — The Roman-era street runs across the valley floor over uneven ancient paving and rubble; long, exposed and bumpy, difficult for wheels and tiring for seniors.
