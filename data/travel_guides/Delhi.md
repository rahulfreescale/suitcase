# Delhi, India — Destination Guide

## Overview
Delhi is India's sprawling capital — a layered, intense, endlessly historic city where
Mughal forts and colonial boulevards meet chaotic bazaars and a gleaming modern metro.
It's overwhelming and magnificent in equal measure, and the gateway to North India.

## Best Time to Visit
October to March is the season — pleasant, cool, and dry. Avoid the brutal summer
(April–June, often 40°C+) and the July–September monsoon. November can bring serious
air pollution, which is worth checking before a trip.

## Neighborhoods
Old Delhi is the dense Mughal heart — Red Fort, Jama Masjid, and the Chandni Chowk
bazaars. New Delhi (Lutyens') has the wide avenues, India Gate, and Connaught Place.
South Delhi (Hauz Khas, Saket) is greener, leafier, and more modern, with malls and
cafes.

## Things to Do
See the Red Fort and Humayun's Tomb, wander (or brave) Chandni Chowk by cycle-rickshaw,
visit the Qutub Minar and the serene Lotus Temple and Akshardham, stroll the Lodhi
Gardens, and eat extraordinary street food and Mughlai cuisine. Delhi is also the launch
point for the Taj Mahal in Agra.

## Getting Around
The Delhi Metro is a genuine triumph — clean, cheap, air-conditioned, and accessible,
covering much of the city. Auto-rickshaws and app cabs (Uber/Ola) handle the last mile.
Traffic is heavy and roads are chaotic; the metro is by far the least stressful way to
move.

## Budget Notes
Delhi is very inexpensive by Western standards — street food, transport, and many
monuments cost little (though foreigner monument tickets are higher than local ones).
The metro and rickshaws are cheap. Splurges are limited to upscale hotels and restaurants.

## Family & Accessibility
Delhi is improving but remains a genuinely challenging city for wheelchair users and
strollers — worth being honest about. The star is the Delhi Metro: elevators and ramps
at stations, braille buttons, tactile paving, wide gates, and dedicated wheelchair spaces
make it one of India's most accessible transit systems. Several major monuments have
added ramps and accessible routes — the Red Fort complex, Qutub Minar (a wheelchair user
can reach the Iron Pillar), and the Humayun's Tomb complex are broadly accessible (though
the actual tomb interiors often are not), and the modern Lotus Temple, Akshardham, Lodhi
Garden, and Gurudwara Bangla Sahib are barrier-free and welcoming. The real difficulties
are between and around sights: pavements are often uneven, broken, or missing curb cuts,
the gap from a metro station to a monument usually means an auto-rickshaw (hard with a
wheelchair), traffic is intense, and Old Delhi's Chandni Chowk is wonderful but extremely
crowded, narrow, and chaotic. Air pollution (worst Oct–Feb) is a real consideration for
toddlers and anyone with respiratory issues. For families, Indian culture is very
child-welcoming, museums like the National Rail Museum and National Science Centre are
kid-friendly and accessible, and the big flat monument gardens (Lodhi, Humayun's) are good
for little ones — but plan for heat, crowds, and dust, and favor a carrier over a stroller
in the old city.

## Popular but Challenging
- Chandni Chowk (Old Delhi bazaars) — the sensory highlight of Delhi, but a chaotic crush of narrow lanes, cycle-rickshaws, wires, and uneven ground; very hard with a stroller or wheelchair. Alt: see it by cycle-rickshaw (the traditional way), or enjoy the calmer Dilli Haat crafts market, which has accessible paths.
- Jama Masjid — India's largest mosque, but reached by steep stone steps with no step-free route to the main courtyard; not wheelchair accessible. Alt: admire it from outside, or visit the accessible Gurudwara Bangla Sahib instead.
- Monument interiors (Humayun's Tomb, Red Fort palaces) — the complexes and gardens are largely accessible, but the raised tomb chambers and some palace interiors have steps. Alt: enjoy the accessible grounds and lower levels.
- Metro-to-sight "last mile" — the metro is superb, but most monuments sit a rickshaw ride from the station over rough roads; a real barrier for wheelchair users. Alt: pre-book an accessible cab or app taxi door-to-door.
- Air pollution season (Oct–Feb) — not a place but a hazard: smog can be severe and is genuinely risky for toddlers. Alt: check air-quality forecasts, favor indoor/morning activities, and consider spring for a family trip.
