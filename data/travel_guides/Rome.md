# Rome, Italy — Destination Guide

## Overview
Rome is Italy's capital and an open-air museum of Western civilization — ancient
ruins, Renaissance art, and Baroque fountains layered across a city that has been
lived in for nearly three thousand years. It rewards curiosity but demands stamina.

## Best Time to Visit
Spring (April–June) and fall (September–October) bring warm, comfortable weather
and lighter crowds than the brutal, packed summer. Winter is mild, quiet, and the
best value, though some days are rainy.

## Neighborhoods
The historic center (centro storico) holds the Pantheon, Piazza Navona, and Trevi
Fountain in a dense, walkable warren of lanes. Trastevere, across the Tiber, is the
bohemian food-and-nightlife quarter. Monti, near the Colosseum, is trendy and
central. The Vatican sits on the west bank, a district of its own.

## Things to Do
See the Colosseum and Roman Forum, throw a coin in the Trevi Fountain, visit the
Pantheon, tour the Vatican Museums and the Sistine Chapel, and climb the dome of
St. Peter's Basilica. Eat cacio e pepe and Roman-style pizza, and take an evening
passeggiata through the piazzas.

## Getting Around
Rome's center is walkable but sprawling, and the ancient cobblestones (sampietrini)
are hard on feet and wheels. The metro (Lines A, B, C) is limited but useful; buses
cover the gaps — ATAC reports 98% of its bus fleet has ramps and wheelchair space.
Main attractions cluster close together, so a central base cuts down on transit.

## Budget Notes
Rome is mid-range: many headline sights (the Pantheon, throwing coins, wandering
the piazzas) are free or cheap, but Vatican and Colosseum tickets, and central
dining, add up. Neighborhood trattorias away from the tourist core are far better value.

## Family & Accessibility
Rome is rewarding but genuinely challenging for wheelchair users and strollers: the
historic center's cobblestones are uneven and often broken, and the city is built on
hills. Accessibility is inconsistent street to street. The good news is that many
major sights are accessible — the Colosseum has ramps and an elevator to the second
level (free entry for a disabled visitor plus companion), the Pantheon is mostly flat
inside, and squares like Piazza Navona, Piazza del Popolo, and the Vatican's St.
Peter's Square have relatively smooth, flat pavements; Via dei Fori Imperiali and Via
del Corso are wide, smooth, and cobble-free. The Roman Forum, by contrast, is only
partly accessible with treacherous uneven ground. For families with toddlers, a
carrier often beats a stroller in the historic core, and the accessible buses (lines
like 157 and 590 have ramps) help avoid the hills. Metro Line A has poor step-free
access; Lines B and C are better.

## Popular but Challenging
- Spanish Steps — iconic, but 135 steps with no step-free route; little for a toddler to do. Alt: the flat Villa Borghese gardens nearby.
- Roman Forum — stunning ruins, but uneven ancient cobbles and only ~10% accessible; bumpy and tiring with a stroller or wheelchair. Alt: view it from above at the flat Campidoglio terrace.
- Trastevere at night — great food and atmosphere, but cobbled, narrow, and crowded after dark; hard with a stroller and past nap/bedtime. Alt: an early family dinner in the flatter centro storico.
- Sistine Chapel to St. Peter's Basilica — the direct route between them has 75+ steps and is not step-free; wheelchair users need the longer accessible path. Alt: ask staff for the accessible route in advance.
- The Pantheon-to-Trevi lane network — beautiful but paved with severe, often-broken cobblestones (sampietrini) around the Pantheon and Trevi; the roughest wheeling in the city.
