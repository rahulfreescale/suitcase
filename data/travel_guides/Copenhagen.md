# Copenhagen, Denmark — Destination Guide

## Overview
Copenhagen is Denmark's design-forward, bicycle-loving capital — a flat, clean,
harbor-side city of colorful facades, hygge cafes, and some of the world's best
public infrastructure. It's welcoming, calm, and genuinely easy to get around.

## Best Time to Visit
Summer (June–August) is the peak, with long daylight, outdoor dining, and Tivoli in
full swing. Late spring and early fall are pleasant and quieter. Winter is dark and
cold but famously cozy, with Christmas markets and candlelit cafes.

## Neighborhoods
Indre By (the old town) holds Nyhavn, Strøget shopping, and the palaces. Nørrebro is
the multicultural, hip district with great food. Vesterbro is trendy and central.
Christianshavn is a canal-laced island neighborhood with a village feel.

## Things to Do
Stroll the colorful Nyhavn harbor, spend an evening at Tivoli Gardens, see the Little
Mermaid, watch the changing of the guard at Amalienborg Palace, explore the National
Museum, and eat your way through Torvehallerne food market. Canal tours are a lovely
introduction to the city.

## Getting Around
Copenhagen is flat and superbly connected: a fully accessible driverless Metro (with
elevators at every station), S-trains, and buses. But the real local mode is the
bicycle — bike lanes are everywhere and often wider than car lanes. Much of the center
is walkable end to end.

## Budget Notes
Copenhagen is expensive — one of Europe's priciest cities for food and lodging. Save
by using the food halls and street food (smørrebrød, hot dogs), enjoying the free
harbor and park spaces, and considering the Copenhagen Card for transport and museum
entry (note it covers Tivoli entry but not the rides).

## Family & Accessibility
Copenhagen is one of Europe's most accessible cities: it's flat, and the Metro is
entirely step-free with elevators at every station and level boarding. Nyhavn's
boardwalk is mostly flat with ramps (a few cobbled stretches can be bumpy), the
Little Mermaid sits on an accessible promenade, and the National Museum and
Amalienborg's courtyard and museum are wheelchair-friendly. Tivoli Gardens is a
standout for families — level paved paths, accessible toilets in every block, free
wheelchair rental with advance booking, and 22 of 28 rides accessible with a companion
transfer; it's genuinely toddler-friendly with a dedicated Rasmus Klump children's
zone, gentle rides, stroller rental, and baby-changing facilities throughout. The city
is very stroller-friendly overall — locals cruise everywhere with prams and cargo bikes.
Two caveats: some historic sites (Rosenborg Castle interior, patches around
Christiansborg) have rough cobbles, and many older restaurants have a step at the door
and lack accessible toilets, so a central base for breaks helps.

## Popular but Challenging
- Rosenborg Castle interior — the historic castle has limited step access inside (the crown jewels are down/up stairs); the surrounding King's Garden, however, is flat and lovely. Alt: enjoy the gardens and skip the interior.
- The Round Tower (Rundetaarn) — famous for its spiral ramp, but it's a long, steep climb; doable for some wheelchair users with effort, tiring with a toddler.
- Christiansborg Palace forecourt — some of the worst cobblestones in the city, rough for wheels for a short stretch.
- Many older restaurants — a large share have at least one step at the door and no accessible toilet, even in touristy areas; book ahead or favor the food halls (Torvehallerne, Reffen) which are flat and easy.
- Nyhavn's cobbled side — the boardwalk is flat, but the cobbled street behind the waterfront can be bumpy; stick to the water side.
