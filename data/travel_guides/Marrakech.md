# Marrakech, Morocco — Destination Guide

## Overview
Marrakech is a sensory North African city of labyrinthine souks, ornate palaces,
tranquil riads, and a famous central square that comes alive at night.

## Best Time to Visit
Spring (March–May) and fall (September–November) are pleasantly warm. Summer is
very hot; winter is mild by day and cold at night.

## Neighborhoods
The Medina (old walled city) holds the souks, palaces, and most riads. Jemaa
el-Fnaa is the central square. Gueliz is the modern district with newer restaurants.
The Palmeraie is a palm-grove resort area outside the center.

## Things to Do
Wander the souks and haggle, visit the Bahia Palace and Ben Youssef Madrasa, relax
in the Majorelle Garden, experience Jemaa el-Fnaa square at dusk, and take a hammam.
Day-trip to the Atlas Mountains or the desert.

## Getting Around
The Medina is walking-only in its narrow lanes — easy to get lost, which is part of
the charm. Petit taxis handle longer trips; agree on fares first.

## Budget Notes
Budget-to-mid-range. Riads, food, and souk goods are inexpensive (bargaining
expected). Desert and mountain excursions are the bigger-ticket items.

## Family & Accessibility
Marrakech's narrow medina lanes are difficult for strollers and wheelchairs, and crowds are intense. Families may prefer riads with courtyards and calmer Gueliz. Gardens like Majorelle are more accessible.

## Popular but Challenging
- The deep souks — the labyrinthine market alleys are the heart of Marrakech, but deeper in they turn narrow, uneven, and intensely crowded (vendors and motorbikes competing for space); strollers are impractical past the wider entrances. Alt: a baby carrier for the deep souks; enjoy the more open Jemaa el-Fnaa square and Kasbah-district streets with wheels.
- Riads (traditional guesthouses) — atmospheric and authentic, but built around courtyards with narrow, steep staircases and rarely a lift; upper rooms are out of reach for wheelchair users. Alt: book a riad with a ground-floor room and wide doors, or a modern hotel in Gueliz/Palmeraie.
- Palaces & multi-level sights — Bahia Palace, El Badi, and the Saadian Tombs are stunning but only partially accessible, with steps and levels connected by stairs. Alt: Bahia Palace and the Jardin Majorelle / Le Jardin Secret are the more accessible choices (still partial — call ahead).
- Getting around by bus — city buses are old, high-floored, and not wheelchair-equipped, and there's no tram (yet). Alt: use the beige "petit taxis" around town and "grand taxis" for day trips; there are only a handful of adapted vehicles, so pre-arrange.
- Roads and heat — a general hazard: potholed, uneven surfaces throughout (manual chairs fare better than power chairs), plus punishing summer heat. Alt: manual wheelchair, plan around the heat, and consider a knowledgeable guide/assistant for the medina.
