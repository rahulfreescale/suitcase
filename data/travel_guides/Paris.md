# Paris, France — Destination Guide

## Overview
Paris is the archetypal European capital — grand boulevards, the Seine, world-defining art, and a cafe on every corner. Compact and dense, it rewards wandering, but its beauty is built on 19th-century stairs and cobbles.

## Best Time to Visit
April to June and September to October offer mild weather and lively streets without peak-summer crowds and heat. August empties as Parisians leave. Winter is grey but atmospheric and cheaper.

## Neighborhoods
The Marais is medieval lanes, boutiques and falafel; Saint-Germain is literary cafes and galleries; Montmartre climbs to Sacré-Cœur; the Latin Quarter is student energy near the Panthéon. The Champs-Élysées and 8th are grand and touristy; Canal Saint-Martin is younger and local.

## Things to Do
Climb or admire the Eiffel Tower, wander the Louvre and Musée d'Orsay, walk Notre-Dame's island and the Seine quays, browse the Marais, and ride to Sacré-Cœur for the view. Eat pastries, sit in cafes, and take an evening river cruise.

## Getting Around
The Métro is fast and cheap but overwhelmingly step-only — very few lines are accessible; line 14 is the exception. Buses are far better: the whole fleet is wheelchair-accessible with ramps. The centre is walkable and flat-ish along the river, but cobbles are common. RER and trams add step-free options.

## Budget Notes
Mid-to-expensive. Many icons (Notre-Dame exterior, Seine walks, Montmartre, parks) are free, and museums are free on some first Sundays. The Louvre, Eiffel summit, and cafe dining add up; bakeries and picnics keep costs down.

## Family & Accessibility
Paris is beautiful but genuinely hard for wheelchair users because the Métro is almost entirely inaccessible — plan around the fully accessible bus network instead. Above ground, the major museums (Louvre, Orsay, Pompidou) are excellent with lifts, free disabled entry and priority access, and the Seine quays and big parks are flat. But Montmartre is steep and stepped, many older streets are cobbled, and historic sites vary. Strollers manage on the boulevards and in parks; the Métro's stairs make a carrier easier in the centre.

## Popular but Challenging
- Sacré-Cœur & Montmartre — The basilica sits atop a steep hill reached by long staircases; a funicular helps but Montmartre's streets are steep and cobbled throughout.
- Arc de Triomphe — Reached by an underground pedestrian passage (there is a lift on request); the rooftop is 284 steps with no public lift to the top.
