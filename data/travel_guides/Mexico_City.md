# Mexico City, Mexico — Destination Guide

## Overview
Mexico City (CDMX) is a vast, vibrant capital with world-class museums, a legendary
food scene, leafy neighborhoods, and deep Aztec and colonial history.

## Best Time to Visit
Spring (March–May) is warm and dry. The rainy season (June–September) brings
afternoon showers. Fall around Día de los Muertos (late October–early November) is
festive and popular.

## Neighborhoods
Roma and Condesa are leafy, walkable, café-filled favorites. Centro Histórico holds
the Zócalo and grand colonial buildings. Coyoacán is bohemian, home to the Frida
Kahlo Museum. Polanco is upscale dining.

## Things to Do
Explore the Zócalo and Templo Mayor, visit the anthropology museum, take a boat
through Xochimilco's canals, day-trip to the Teotihuacan pyramids, and eat tacos,
tamales, and mole across the city.

## Getting Around
The metro is cheap and extensive; ride-hailing apps are affordable and convenient.
Roma and Condesa are best explored on foot.

## Budget Notes
Budget-to-mid-range. Street food and markets are very cheap; upscale Polanco dining
runs high. Excellent value overall for a major capital.

## Family & Accessibility
Mexico City's historic center is walkable and stroller-friendly; families love Chapultepec Park, the zoo, and Xochimilco boats. Accessibility varies — newer museums are well-equipped, older sites less so.

## Popular but Challenging
- Teotihuacán pyramids — the day-trip highlight, but you can't climb the pyramids with a toddler (steep and dangerous) or in a wheelchair, and the Avenue of the Dead is a dusty, dirt-and-gravel path with barrier walls and stairs every ~100 yards for 2 miles. Alt: a carrier not a stroller; enter at the accessible Gate 3/5 near the Pyramid of the Sun, see the pyramids from the base, and visit the accessible site museum — or save the climb for when kids are older.
- Coyoacán's cobbled streets — the bohemian Frida Kahlo district is charming but cobblestoned and uneven; bumpy with a stroller or wheelchair. Alt: stick to the main plazas and the accessible museum interiors.
- Xochimilco trajineras — the colorful canal boats are a classic, but boarding the flat-bottomed boats involves a step down and no ramp. Alt: some operators help with boarding; go with assistance, or enjoy the accessible lakeside.
- Templo Mayor ruins — the Aztec ruins by the Zócalo are walked via raised metal walkways with steps in places; only partially accessible. Alt: the adjacent museum is accessible and tells the same story; view the ruins from accessible vantage points.
- Altitude, sun, and traffic — a general hazard: 2,240 m elevation tires little legs, Teotihuacán has zero shade, and traffic makes transfers long. Alt: Uber is the recommended way to get around for wheelchair users; pace days, hydrate, and pack sun protection.
