# Nairobi, Kenya — Destination Guide

## Overview
Nairobi is Kenya's vibrant capital and the gateway to East Africa's safaris — a fast-growing,
high-energy city with the remarkable distinction of a national park full of wildlife right on
its edge. It blends urban bustle, green suburbs, and unrivaled access to Africa's iconic
animals.

## Best Time to Visit
The dry seasons — June to October and January to February — are best for wildlife and
comfortable weather. The "long rains" (March–May) and "short rains" (November–December) bring
greener landscapes but wetter game drives. Nairobi's highland altitude keeps it mild
year-round.

## Neighborhoods
The Central Business District is the busy downtown core. Karen and Langata, leafy southern
suburbs, hold the famous wildlife attractions (Giraffe Centre, Blixen house) and are calmer
and greener. Westlands and Gigiri are the upscale, international dining-and-shopping districts.

## Things to Do
Feed giraffes at the Giraffe Centre, watch orphaned elephants at the Sheldrick Wildlife Trust,
go on a game drive in Nairobi National Park (lions and rhinos against the skyline), visit the
Karen Blixen Museum and Bomas of Kenya, and use the city as a launchpad for Maasai Mara and
Amboseli safaris.

## Getting Around
Nairobi has buses and matatus (minibuses), but for visitors, taxis and ride-hailing (Uber/Bolt)
are the practical, affordable way around — traffic is notoriously heavy. Most sightseeing
(especially the wildlife attractions in Karen/Langata) is done by car or organized tour rather
than public transit.

## Budget Notes
Nairobi is moderate: local food and transport are cheap, while safaris, park fees (Nairobi
National Park charges a notable conservancy fee), and upscale lodges/hotels are the big costs.
The wildlife attractions have modest entry fees. Booking day tours can be efficient value.

## Family & Accessibility
Nairobi is a rewarding but practically challenging destination — the magic is the wildlife, and
much of it is more accessible than you'd expect, while general city infrastructure is limited.
The headline attractions have made real efforts: the Giraffe Centre has wheelchair ramps,
accessible restrooms, and a raised feeding platform (a thrill for kids and easy for wheelchair
users), the Sheldrick Elephant Orphanage and Nairobi National Museum have paved paths and
ramps, and specialist operators run genuinely wheelchair- and stroller-accessible safari vans
and tours (Nairobi National Park game drives can be done from an accessible vehicle). Kids adore
it — feeding giraffes and seeing baby elephants is pure magic, and several sites are cheap or
free for under-3s. The challenges are real: city pavements are uneven and often lack curb cuts,
traffic is heavy (budget lots of time), safari terrain is naturally rough, and public transit
isn't practical for visitors — you'll rely on cars/tours. For families, base in the greener
Karen/Langata area near the wildlife sites, use a trusted driver or tour operator, and expect a
car-centric, tour-led trip rather than independent city wandering.

## Popular but Challenging
- Nairobi National Park game drive — unmissable, but standard safari vehicles and rough park tracks aren't wheelchair-friendly, and long drives test toddlers. Alt: book a specialist operator's accessible safari van, and keep drives shorter with young kids.
- Chandni-Chowk-style CBD streets (downtown Nairobi) — chaotic, crowded, and with uneven, curb-cut-poor pavements; hard and not especially rewarding with a stroller or wheelchair. Alt: focus on the Karen/Langata wildlife sites and green spaces instead.
- Sheldrick Elephant Orphanage viewing — wonderful, but the single daily public hour draws crowds and the viewing area ground can be uneven/muddy after rain. Alt: arrive early for a front spot; wear sturdy footwear and manage the timing with kids.
- Getting between sights — not a place but the core difficulty: heavy traffic and long transfers over rough roads, with no practical accessible public transit. Alt: hire a reliable driver/tour for the day and cluster the Karen-area sights together.
- Bomas of Kenya / open-ground cultural sites — great for kids, but some areas are on grass/dirt that's tricky for wheels. Alt: stick to the paved seating and main paths.
