# Montreal, Canada — Destination Guide

## Overview
Montréal is French-speaking Canada's cultural heart — a European-flavored island city of cobbled old quarters, a mountain park, festival squares, and a legendary food scene. Old Montréal charms but cobbles; the modern grid and Underground City are easier.

## Best Time to Visit
Summer (June–September) is festival season and the city's joyful peak, warm and buzzing. Fall is crisp and colorful. Winter is long, cold and snowy — but the vast Underground City keeps downtown moving indoors.

## Neighborhoods
Old Montréal (Vieux-Montréal) is cobbled 17th-century streets and the waterfront. Downtown has skyscrapers and the Underground City; the Plateau and Mile End are bohemian, muraled and food-rich; Little Italy has the Jean-Talon Market; the Village is lively and inclusive.

## Things to Do
Wander Old Montréal and its basilica, climb (or ride) up Mont-Royal for the city view, browse the Jean-Talon and Atwater markets, visit the Museum of Fine Arts, and eat bagels, smoked meat and poutine. Time it for a summer festival if you can.

## Getting Around
The Métro is clean and fast but only partly accessible — a limited number of stations have elevators, so check; buses are fully accessible with ramps. Downtown is flat with the step-free Underground City; Old Montréal is cobbled and uneven; the Plateau has some hills and outdoor staircases. Winter adds ice and snow.

## Budget Notes
Mid-range and often better value than Toronto. Markets, Old Montréal, Mont-Royal and festivals are largely free; the Museum of Fine Arts has free collection access. Bagels, poutine and market food are cheap; the bigger costs are dining out and winter activities.

## Family & Accessibility
Montréal is a mixed picture. Downtown is flat with the enormous step-free Underground City (elevators throughout) — a genuine winter asset — and the Museum of Fine Arts and major venues are accessible. But Old Montréal is paved in historic cobblestones that are rough for wheels and strollers, the Métro has limited elevator coverage, and Mont-Royal and the Plateau have hills and outdoor stairs. Buses are the reliable step-free option. Manageable downtown; harder in the old and hilly quarters.

## Popular but Challenging
- Old Montréal (Vieux-Montréal) — Beautiful historic quarter paved in uneven cobblestones with some slopes down to the waterfront; charming but jarring for wheels and strollers, though shops and the waterfront promenade are flatter.
- Mont-Royal Park — The mountain park has some accessible paved paths and a shuttle/road to the Kondiaronk lookout, but many trails are steep gravel or stairs.
