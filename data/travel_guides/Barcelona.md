# Barcelona, Spain — Destination Guide

## Overview
Barcelona blends Mediterranean beaches, Gaudí's surreal architecture, and a
buzzing food-and-nightlife scene. It's walkable, sunny, and design-obsessed.

## Best Time to Visit
Late spring (May–June) and early fall (September) are warm without peak-summer
crowds and heat. July–August is hot and crowded but full of beach energy.

## Neighborhoods
The Gothic Quarter (Barri Gòtic) is medieval and maze-like. El Born is trendy and
historic. Eixample holds Gaudí's landmarks and grid streets. Barceloneta is the
beach district. Gràcia is the bohemian, local-feeling area.

## Things to Do
See the Sagrada Família and Park Güell (book ahead), stroll Las Ramblas, eat tapas
in El Born, relax on Barceloneta beach, and take the funicular up Montjuïc for views.

## Getting Around
Compact and walkable, with an easy metro. Beaches and hilltop sights are a short
ride away. Watch for pickpockets in tourist-dense areas.

## Budget Notes
Mid-range. Tapas and menú del día lunches are good value; beachfront and Gaudí
sights cost more. Book major attractions in advance to avoid premium resale prices.

## Family & Accessibility
Barcelona is stroller-friendly along the flat Eixample grid and beachfront; the Gothic Quarter's lanes are tighter. Park Güell and the aquarium are family favorites. Many attractions offer wheelchair access.

## Popular but Challenging
- Sagrada Família towers — the basilica's ground floor is fully step-free (free entry for disabled visitors + companion, via Entrance B on Carrer de la Marina), but the towers are firmly off-limits to wheelchairs and strollers: a lift goes up but descent is by narrow stairs only. Alt: enjoy the spectacular accessible nave and museum; skip the towers.
- Park Güell — Gaudí's mosaic park is on a steep hill, partly unpaved, with numerous stairs; genuinely hard with a stroller or manual wheelchair even with the ramps and escalators. Alt: enter from the Carretera del Carmel side (bus 24/116) to minimize climbing, or admire from the more accessible lower terraces.
- Montjuïc cable car (Telefèric) — great views, but the harbour gondola has steps at both ends and isn't wheelchair accessible. Alt: reach Montjuïc's accessible sights by adapted taxi or bus instead.
- Attraction queues (Sagrada Família, Picasso Museum) — the sights are accessible but the lines are punishing in peak season and hard with toddlers. Alt: pre-book timed tickets online (disabled tariff skips the line) and go early on a weekday.
- Distances between sights — a hidden trap: Park Güell is 4+ km from the Gothic Quarter, so "walkable Barcelona" still means transit between clusters. Alt: cluster sights by area and use the mostly-accessible metro (most stations have lifts) or accessible taxis (Taxi Amic).
